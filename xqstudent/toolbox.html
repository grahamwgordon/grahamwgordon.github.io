Given a real number \(a\), its additive inverse is \(-a\). When added, they produce the additive identity, which is zero. In symbols, \(a + (-a) = 0\) for all real numbers \(a\).
Given a nonzero real number \(a\), its multiplicative inverse is \(\frac{1}{a}\). When multiplied, they produce the multiplicative identity, which is one. In symboles, \(a \cdot \frac{1}{a} = 1\).

1. Find somewhere a zero is being added to or subtracted from a quantity. \(\underbrace{3x + 0}_{\text{here!}} - x^2\)
2. Replace the sum or difference with the nonzero quantity. \(3x - x^2\)
3. The result is equivalent to what you started with. \(3x + 0 - x^2 = 3x - x^2\)

or
1. Find a quantity that is being multiplied or divided by one. \(\underbrace{1 \times 2}_{\text{here!}} + 3\)
2. Replace the product or quotient with the quantity that isn't (necessarily) one. \(2 + 3\)
3. The result is equivalent to what you started with. \(1 \times 2 + 3 = 2 + 3\)

The phrase gather like terms emcompasses may of the other tools on this page, including associativity, commutativity, and factoring. At the core of this process is doing things like rewriting \(x^2 + 3x^2\) as \(4x^2\), and also leaving things like \(x^2 - y\) alone. What's going on "under the hood" is that like terms must have a variable portion that is always equal, no matter what the values of the variables are. That's why like terms must have the same variables (\(x\) and \(y\) don't always have to be equal to each other) raised to the same powers (\(x\) and \(x^2\) don't have to be equal to each other).

The mean (also known as the average) of a list of numbers is a measure of the center or position of the numbers. Somewhat informally, it represents a "typical" value from the list if you were to pick at random. More precisely, if every number in the list were replaced by the mean, the sum of the numbers would stay the same. For this reason, the mean is skewed toward outliers. For a measure of center that is not skewed by outliers, check out the median.
