As a strategy for solving systems of equations, substitution is a combination of isolating variables and using substitution for creating equivalent expressions. That's all!

1. Start with a system of equations. \(\left \{ \begin{aligned} x+y & = 10 \\ 2x+y & = 12 \end{aligned} \right .\)
2. Pick one of the variables to eliminate, and create equivalent equations so that the variable is on the same side of each equation, but has opposite coefficients. \(\left \{ \begin{aligned} -x-y & = -10 \\ 2x + y & = 12 \end{aligned} \right .\)
3. Add the lefthand sides of the equations, and also add the righthand sides. This is still valid because you added equivalent quantities to both sides. \((-x-y)+(2x+y) = -10 + 12\)
4. Isolate the other variable (not the one you eliminated) in this new equation. \(-x+2x-y+y=-10+12 \rightarrow x = 2\)
5. Solve for the variable you eliminated with one of the equations you started with. \(x+y = 10 \rightarrow 2+y=10 \rightarrow y=8\)

The mean (also known as the average) of a list of numbers is a measure of the center or position of the numbers. Somewhat informally, it represents a "typical" value from the list if you were to pick at random. More precisely, if every number in the list were replaced by the mean, the sum of the numbers would stay the same. For this reason, the mean is skewed toward outliers. For a measure of center that is not skewed by outliers, check out the median.

1. Start with a list of numbers. \(1,3,4,4,2\)
2. Order them from least to greatest. \(1,2,3,4,4\)
3. Repeatedly cross off the smallest and largest numbers until 1 or 2 numbers remain. \(\require{cancel} \cancel{1}, \cancel{2}, 3, \cancel{4}, \cancel{4}\)
4. If one number remains, it is the median. \(3\)
5. If two numbers remain, their average is the median. \(1,2,3,7 \rightarrow \cancel{1},2,3,\cancel{7} \rightarrow \frac{2+3}{2} = 2.5\)

The interquartile range, like the standard deviation, is a measure of spread. However, unlike the standard deviation, the interquartile range is better suited for asymmetric of heavily skewed data.

4. Data points outside of that range of values are considered outliers. \(45\) is the only outlier.

Outliers are data points that appear to fall well beyond the typical range of data points in a data set. There is no universal precise way to identify outliers, but one that is commonly used is the \(1.5 \cdot \text{IQR}\) rule. It suggests considering \(x\) and outlier if either \(x < \text{Q}1 - 1.5 \cdot \text{IQR}\) or \(x > \text{Q}3 + 1.5 \cdot \text{IQR}\).

The standard deviation of a data set measures how much the data points vary from the mean. There are two slightly different ways of computing it. In each case, let \(x_1,x_2,\ldots,x_n\) be the collection of \(n\) data points you are pondering.

  • If you know the mean of the population you got your data from, then you can compute the population standard deviation as follows. Let \(\mu\) denote the mean of the population. Then the population standard deviation is given by the following formula. \[ \sigma_{\text{population}} = \sqrt{\frac{(x_1-\mu)^2 + \cdots + (x_n - \mu)^2 }{n}} \]
  • Even if you don't know the mean of the population, you can still compute the sample standard deviation as follows. Let \(\bar{x}\) denote the mean of the data set \(x_1, \ldots, x_n\). Then the sample standard deviation is given by the following formula. \[ \sigma_{\text{sample}} = \sqrt{\frac{(x_1-\bar{x})^2 + \cdots + (x_n - \bar{x})^2 }{n-1}} \]
